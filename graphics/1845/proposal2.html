Final Project Proposal

Minnie Wongpanich & Owen Jow

We intend to create an interactive water scene that the user can manipulate. The primary focus of our project (tentatively titled "H2O") is to realistically simulate the properties of a fluid such as water and demonstrate how it interacts with its environment. In order to do this, we will model our system as a collection of particles and implement a lot of physically-based mathematics.

Background

As has already been mentioned, realistic fluid simulation is the main problem we want to solve. We're interested in fluid simulation since it's an area of graphics that has proven to be pretty challenging to model and render. Fluids are a key component for many of the visual effects we see in movies, games, and art, and being able to look at realistically rendered fluids really enhances our enjoyment of these media. Fluid simulation also has applications outside of visual effects â€“ in weather modeling, for example, alongside such things as ocean current simulation and aerodynamics.

Our inspiration for this project comes from Evan Wallace's water demo, which he implemented using WebGL. We also plan to reference Miles Macklin and Matthias Müller's paper on position based fluids (PBF).

Goals and Deliverables

  1. What we plan to deliver

    • A video of our fluid behaving in changing environments at varying particle counts. The following fluid dynamics video by Miles Macklin (author of the PBF paper) is an example of a demo we can strive to emulate:
    • Graphs comparing the speed of our renderer with different numbers of particles simulated.
  2. What we hope to deliver

    3. If we manage to complete our fluid simulation with time to spare, we also hope to bring it up to interactive speeds so that the user can directly change the environment of the fluid and see how it reacts. Deliverables in this ambitious scenario:

    • An interactive demo of our fluid simulator, in which the user can:
      • Change the camera angle
      • Move objects that are in the fluid, or shift the walls of the fluid's container
      • Change the angle of the light source
    • If we still have extra time after this, we could also implement caustics caused by the water.
